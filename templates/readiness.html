What is AI Readiness?

AI readiness is about how prepared your institution is to work with AI across what it does: teaching, research, governance, student support. It looks different at every campus because every campus is starting from a different place, with different students, resources, and priorities.

The challenge institutions are facing

Everyone is rethinking how they work

Institutions have decades of experience managing technology change, but AI is different. It's pushing every campus to rethink established practices at the same time. Faculty are experimenting with new teaching approaches. Leaders are drafting policies for tools that didn't exist a year ago. Much of this is happening ad hoc, and best practices are still taking shape. The challenge isn't starting from zero. It's that everyone is figuring it out in parallel, with no shared playbook yet.

No common way to talk about it

Without shared language, institutions can't learn from each other. A department chair in Texas and one in Ohio might both be redesigning curricula around AI, but they have no way to compare approaches. Leaders can't benchmark where they stand. Staff can't point to a common standard when proposing new tools. The conversations are happening everywhere, just not across campuses.

A framework built for this moment

AIREX organizes AI readiness into five pillars and four developmental levels. The pillars cover the core areas where AI touches institutional life. The levels describe where an institution might be in each area, from early exploration to deep integration.

Five pillars

The core areas where AI touches what your institution does.

Teaching & Learning

AI in curriculum design, faculty development, student preparation, and classroom integration. How is AI shaping what and how students learn?

Policy & Governance

Institutional AI strategy, acceptable use policies, data governance, and leadership support. Does your institution have a clear AI direction?

Ethics & Equity

Responsible AI practices, bias awareness, equitable access, and inclusive adoption. Are AI benefits reaching all students and staff?

Research & Innovation

AI-assisted research, faculty adoption, partnerships, and community impact. How is AI advancing your institution's research mission?

Infrastructure

Computing resources, data systems, technical staffing, security, and support services. Does your institution have the technical foundation for AI?

How progress is measured

Four AI Readiness levels

The framework describes four stages of progress, from early conversations to deep integration. They reflect where an institution is in the process, not how well it's performing.

Exploring

Aware of AI's potential. Early conversations are happening. No formal strategy yet, but curiosity and interest are growing.

Building

Pilot initiatives underway. Policies are being drafted. Some faculty are adopting AI tools. A foundation is forming.

Advancing

Coordinated AI strategy in place. Cross-departmental adoption. Dedicated resources and growing institutional support.

Leading

AI is deeply embedded in institutional operations. Contributing to the broader field. Mentoring peer institutions.

Why we built it this way

A few decisions shaped how this framework works, and why.

Designed to encourage open exchange

We use words like "Exploring" and "Building" instead of scores that feel like grades. There are no rankings and no leaderboards. We found that people engage more honestly when they're not worried about how the results look, so we designed the whole experience around that.

Rooted in what academic institutions are actually facing

The AI Readiness framework grew out of a partnership between AAII, CAHSI and HACU. The pillars, levels, and assessment questions come from working directly with Hispanic-Serving Institutions across 30 states and territories, where AI adoption plays out differently than it does at well-resourced research universities.

Open, and getting better over time

AIREX is open and free for any institution to use. We also built it so the framework can be refined by the people using it. Institutions in the consortium have a direct role in shaping how readiness is defined, what gets measured, and how results get used.

What happens when institutions share a framework

A readiness score is useful on its own. When hundreds of institutions use the same framework, you also start learning from each other.

Shared language

When hundreds of institutions describe AI readiness using the same five pillars and four levels, they can actually talk to each other about it. A provost in Texas and a dean in California can compare notes on infrastructure without spending the first 20 minutes defining terms.

Peer learning

The Atlas maps every consortium institution by readiness level, region, and pillar strengths. Instead of hiring consultants or reading generic reports, you can look at what a peer institution with a similar profile is doing differently on a specific pillar.

Collective intelligence

As more institutions complete the assessment, patterns start to emerge. Which pillars are most institutions scoring low on? Where are the bright spots? What kinds of institutions are moving fastest on ethics or infrastructure? Those patterns help the consortium decide where to put its energy.

Who this is for

Different roles use the framework differently. Here's what that looks like.

Institutional Leadership

Use the five pillars to structure board-level conversations, set AI priorities across divisions, and see where your institution stands relative to peers in the consortium.

Faculty

See where your teaching and research fit into the bigger institutional picture. The pillars help you find where support already exists, where the gaps are, and where working with colleagues could move things forward.

Staff and Administrators

Turn what you see every day into structured evidence. IT directors, advisors, and operations staff can point to specific pillar scores when advocating for resources or planning professional development.
