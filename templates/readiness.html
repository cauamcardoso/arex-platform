What is AI Readiness?

AI readiness is an institution's capacity to intentionally and equitably integrate AI across its core functions. It is not about being first or having the most resources. It is about understanding where you are and having a clear path forward.

A shared language for a shared challenge

AI is changing how institutions teach, research, govern, and serve students. But without a common framework, it is difficult to know where to start or how to measure progress. AIREX defines AI readiness across five pillars and four developmental levels, giving institutions a structured way to understand their current state and plan their next steps.

Who this is for

AI readiness is not a single conversation. It touches different roles in different ways. This framework is designed to meet each audience where they are.

🎓

Institutional Leadership

Presidents, provosts, and deans use the framework to understand institutional positioning, set strategic priorities, and make informed decisions about AI investment. The five-pillar structure offers a clear lens for board-level conversations and cross-institutional comparison.

📚

Faculty

Faculty use the framework to situate their own teaching and research within a broader institutional context. Whether exploring AI-assisted pedagogy or rethinking curriculum design, the pillars help identify where support and collaboration opportunities exist.

⚙️

Staff and Administrators

IT directors, academic advisors, and operations staff use the framework to evaluate infrastructure needs, plan training programs, and advocate for resources. The assessment translates daily observations into structured evidence for decision-makers.

Five pillars of AI readiness

Each pillar represents a core function where AI integration matters. Together, they provide a complete picture of institutional readiness.

📚

Teaching & Learning

AI in curriculum design, faculty development, student preparation, and classroom integration. How is AI shaping what and how students learn?

📋

Policy & Governance

Institutional AI strategy, acceptable use policies, data governance, and leadership support. Does your institution have a clear AI direction?

⚖️

Ethics & Equity

Responsible AI practices, bias awareness, equitable access, and inclusive adoption. Are AI benefits reaching all students and staff?

🔬

Research & Innovation

AI-assisted research, faculty adoption, partnerships, and community impact. How is AI advancing your institution's research mission?

🖥️

Infrastructure

Computing resources, data systems, technical staffing, security, and support services. Does your institution have the technical foundation for AI?

Four developmental levels

AI readiness is a journey, not a destination. Every institution starts somewhere, and each level represents meaningful progress. The language is intentionally growth-oriented.

🔍

Exploring

1.0 – 2.0

Aware of AI's potential. Early conversations are happening. No formal strategy yet, but curiosity and interest are growing.

🔨

Building

2.1 – 3.0

Pilot initiatives underway. Policies are being drafted. Some faculty are adopting AI tools. A foundation is forming.

🚀

Advancing

3.1 – 4.0

Coordinated AI strategy in place. Cross-departmental adoption. Dedicated resources and growing institutional support.

🏛️

Leading

4.1 – 5.0

AI is deeply embedded in institutional operations. Contributing to the broader field. Mentoring peer institutions.

These levels are not rankings. An institution that is Exploring is not failing. It is simply earlier in its journey. Every level represents valid, important work.

How the score works

The assessment is designed to be transparent, accessible, and consistent across institution types.

25
Questions

Five questions for each of the five pillars. Every question maps directly to a pillar, so your results reflect specific areas of strength and growth.

1 to 5
Response Scale

Each question is answered on a 1-to-5 scale. Responses describe observable practices and behaviors rather than aspirational goals.

5
Pillar Scores

Each pillar score is the average of its five questions. A pillar score of 3.4 means your responses averaged 3.4 across that area.

1
Overall Score

Your overall readiness score is the average of all five pillar scores. Every pillar carries equal weight in the calculation.

Equal Weighting

All five pillars contribute equally to the overall score. This reflects the principle that AI readiness requires balanced progress across teaching, policy, ethics, research, and infrastructure.

Anonymous and Accessible

No login required. No data is stored on any server. The assessment takes roughly 10 minutes. All responses stay in your browser and are not transmitted or saved.

Growth-Oriented Design

The 1-to-5 scale uses descriptive language that reflects a progression of practice, not a judgment of quality. A score of 1 means "early stage," not "falling behind." The language is designed to encourage honest self-reflection.

How to use your results

A readiness score is a starting point, not a conclusion. Here is how to translate your results into action.

🔍

Identify your pillar gaps

Look at which pillars scored lower relative to others. These are areas where targeted investment or conversation could make a meaningful difference.

🌎

Find peer institutions on the Atlas

Use the Atlas to find institutions at a similar readiness level or with strengths in your weaker areas. Their profiles may reveal initiatives worth adapting.

💬

Explore the Repository

Search for curated resources filtered by pillar. Syllabi, policy templates, and guides contributed by the network are available to support your next steps.

🔁

Reassess periodically

AI readiness is not static. As your institution evolves, take the assessment again to track progress and surface new areas for development.

💡

A note on our approach

The AIREX framework uses growth-oriented language deliberately. Terms like "Exploring" and "Building" replace evaluative labels because institutions at different stages are doing different kinds of important work. A score is not a grade. There are no rankings, no leaderboards, and no public comparisons. The purpose of this tool is self-reflection and peer learning, not competition.

This design choice reflects a core belief: institutions are more likely to engage honestly and make meaningful progress when the process feels supportive rather than punitive.
